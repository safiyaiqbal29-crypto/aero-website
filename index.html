Aerodynamics of Wings

Aerodynamics is the branch of physics concerned with the interaction between air and moving bodies. Its principles govern how aircraft generate lift, overcome drag, and sustain controlled flight. Any object travelling through the atmosphere — from a rocket at launch to a kite in steady wind — is subject to aerodynamic forces.

The design of an aircraft’s wings plays a crucial role in determining its lift, stability, and overall aerodynamic efficiency

What is Lift?

Lift is the upward force generated when air flows over an aircraft wing. Pressure differences between the top and bottom of the wing create this force.

Wing Design's and Airfoils

Thickness

The thickness of a wing affects the structural strength, the drag and the internal space. In the early 1900’s Ludwig Prandtl and his research team built wind tunnels to test the effects of the thickness of wings. They found that thick wings are stronger and good for low-speed aircraft, however, they produce more drag at high speeds. They also found that thin wings produce less drag which is better for high-speed aircraft, however, they have less internal space.

Camber

Camber is how curved the wing is. In the early 1900’s, engineers such as the Wright brothers (USA) tested how different aerofoils performed in wind tunnels. They found that when the upper surface is more curved, the lift increases. This is because it helps create a pressure difference between the top and bottom of the wing. Therefore, the higher the camber, the higher the lift at lower speeds.

Different Airfoil shapes

Airfoils can vary in camber, thickness, leading and trailing edge shapes. Some of the most common types are; high-lift airfoils, laminar airfoils and supersonic airfoils. High-lift airfoils are very cambered, so they are mainly used for low-speed aircraft. Laminar airfoils are specially designed to reduce drag, resulting in a smooth airflow over the large part of the wing. Lastly, supersonic airfoils are very thin and almost flat in order to reduce shock waves

Types of Aircraft Wings

Swept Wings

Used on high-speed aircraft and fighter jets to reduce drag.

High Aspect Ratio Wings

Long wings used on gliders for maximum lift and efficiency.

Delta Wings

Triangular wings used on some supersonic aircraft.

Glossary

  • Lift – Upward aerodynamic force.
  • Drag – Force opposing motion through air.
  • Airfoil – Cross-sectional shape of a wing.
  • Aspect Ratio – Ratio of wing span to wing width.
